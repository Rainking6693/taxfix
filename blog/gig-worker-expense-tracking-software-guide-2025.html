üöó Rideshare (Uber, Lyft)

  • Vehicle expenses: Gas, oil changes, tire replacements, car washes
  • Phone accessories: Car mounts, chargers, phone upgrades
  • Vehicle accessories: Seat covers, floor mats, air fresheners
  • Tolls and parking: Business-related fees

Key Tracking Features Needed:

  • Automatic mileage detection with trip start/stop
  • Integration with Uber/Lyft driver apps
  • Vehicle expense categorization
  • Real-time gas price optimization

üí° Pro Tip for Rideshare Drivers

Track your mileage from the moment you turn on driver mode until you turn it off, including miles driven to pickup locations and between rides. This typically adds 20-30% more deductible miles compared to just tracking passenger miles.

üõí Delivery (DoorDash, Instacart, UberEats)

Tax Implications & IRS Requirements for Gig Worker Expenses

Understanding IRS rules for gig worker expenses is crucial for maximizing deductions while staying compliant. Here's what you need to know:

üìã IRS Documentation Requirements

Receipts & Records

  • $75+ expenses: Must have receipt or invoice
  • Under $75: Can use credit card statements if business purpose is clear
  • Mileage: Must log date, destination, business purpose, and miles
  • Mixed-use items: Must calculate and document business percentage

Substantiation Rules

  • Amount: Exact dollar amount of expense
  • Time: Date expense was incurred
  • Place: Where expense occurred
  • Business purpose: How it relates to your gig work
  • Business relationship: For meals and entertainment

Record Retention

  • General rule: Keep records for 3 years after filing
  • If underreported income: 6 years
  • Fraudulent returns: Indefinitely
  • Digital copies: Acceptable if clear and legible

üí∞ Common Deduction Categories & Limits

Vehicle Expenses

Standard mileage rate (2023): $0.655 per business mile

Actual expense method: Track all vehicle costs and apply business use percentage

Choose method at start: Can't switch methods for same vehicle

Home Office Deduction

Simplified method: $5 per sq ft up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max)

Actual expense method: Calculate actual costs based on percentage of home used

Regular and exclusive use required: Space must be used only for business

Equipment & Technology

Section 179 deduction: Up to $1,080,000 for qualifying equipment (2023)

Depreciation: Spread cost over useful life for expensive items

Business use percentage: Apply percentage if used for personal activities

Business Meals

2023 rate: 50% deductible for business meals

100% deductible: Company parties, picnics, employee meals

Documentation required: Business purpose and attendees

‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags to Avoid

  • Excessive deductions: Deductions much higher than industry averages
  • Round numbers: Suspiciously round expense amounts
  • Missing documentation: Claims without proper records
  • Personal vs. business confusion: Deducting clearly personal expenses
  • Hobby vs. business: Must show profit motive and business-like operations

Common Expense Tracking Mistakes to Avoid

Learning from others' mistakes can save you time, money, and potential IRS problems. Here are the most common pitfalls gig workers face:

‚ùå Mixing Personal and Business Expenses

The Problem: Using the same credit card or bank account for personal and business expenses makes tracking difficult and increases audit risk.

The Solution:

  • Open separate business checking account and credit card
  • Use business cards exclusively for business expenses
  • If you must mix, document business percentage clearly
  • Review monthly statements to catch any misclassified expenses

Cost of this mistake: $500-$2,000 in missed deductions annually, plus potential audit complications

‚ùå Inconsistent Mileage Tracking

The Problem: Starting and stopping mileage tracking inconsistently, or forgetting to log business purpose for trips.

The Solution:

  • Use automatic GPS tracking software
  • Set phone reminders to start tracking when beginning work
  • Log business purpose immediately, not days later
  • Review and classify trips weekly, not monthly

Cost of this mistake: $1,200-$3,000 in missed mileage deductions for active drivers

‚ùå Poor Receipt Management

The Problem: Losing receipts, waiting too long to scan them, or not capturing enough detail for business justification.

The Solution:

  • Scan receipts immediately using your phone
  • Take photos of both sides of receipts
  • Add voice memos explaining business purpose
  • Set up automatic cloud backup for receipt images

Cost of this mistake: $300-$800 in lost deductions, plus audit vulnerability

‚ùå Ignoring Partial Business Use

The Problem: Not tracking business percentage for mixed-use items like phones, internet, vehicles, or home office space.

The Solution:

  • Calculate and document business use percentage
  • Track usage monthly to establish consistent patterns
  • Use time-tracking data to support percentages
  • Review and adjust percentages annually

Cost of this mistake: $400-$1,200 in missed partial deductions

‚ùå Waiting Until Tax Season

The Problem: Trying to reconstruct a year's worth of expenses from bank statements and faded receipts in January.

The Solution:

  • Track expenses in real-time throughout the year
  • Review and categorize expenses weekly
  • Run quarterly reports to check progress
  • Make estimated tax payments based on current data

Cost of this mistake: 15-25 hours of additional work, plus 20-30% missed deductions

‚ùå Over-Deducting or Under-Documenting

The Problem: Claiming deductions that seem too good to be true, or lacking proper documentation to support legitimate claims.

The Solution:

  • Research IRS guidelines for your specific deductions
  • Maintain detailed records for all business expenses
  • Be conservative with aggressive deductions
  • Consider professional tax advice for complex situations

Cost of this mistake: Potential audit, penalties, interest, and professional fees
